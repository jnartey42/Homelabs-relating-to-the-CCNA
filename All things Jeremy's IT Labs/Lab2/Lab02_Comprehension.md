Interfaces and Cables

Jeremy's IT Labs on YouTube

Comprehension: This video, second in the series, is focused on the different types of cables used such as UTP cables, crossover cables, single mode and multimode fiber cables, interface ports such as RJ-45 connectors, alongside internet speeds that are defined in the IEEE 802.3 standard. I learned how straight-through cables are utilized when connecting two different devices to each other, such as a computer to a switch, for example. Most devices, such as a router, firewall, and PCs, transmit data on pins 1 & 2, and receive on pins 3 & 6, when using fast ethernet speeds, such as 100BASE-T. Switches are the exception, transmitting on 3 & 6 instead. Connecting between two of the same device, such as 2 routers, require the usage of crossover cables. Newer devices should be equipped with AUTO MDI-X, so that you don't have to manually worry about cables going into the wrong interface ports. When wiring up these connections, its important to use the right cables, as some are built for certain distances, whilst others aren't.

Lab: Once again, using Jeremy's lab on Cisco Packet Tracer, largely involved wiring up two different networks, while following the necessary requirements, such as what cables to use when connecting two different devices, or two of the same ones, or across a certain distance. Dark solid connections represent connection between different devices, hashed are for similar devices, such as a router to a router, whilst the red solid ones represent two different networks connecting over a large distance (3 kilometers). In reality, one would use single mode fiber for connecting multiple networks over a long distance, as they're cheaper to implement.
